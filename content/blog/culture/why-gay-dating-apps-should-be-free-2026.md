## The Economics of Gay Dating Apps

The top gay dating apps make billions annually. Grindr alone reported $280 million in revenue in 2024, almost entirely from subscriptions. Tinder (which includes gay users) made $1.9 billion.

Where does that money go? Not to better matching algorithms. Not to safety features. It goes to shareholders.

The apps have discovered that loneliness is incredibly profitable. The more frustrated you are with free limits, the more likely you are to pay. It's a business model built on disappointment.

## What "Premium" Actually Gets You

Let's break down what you get when you pay for premium on major gay dating apps:

- **See who liked you** — This should be free. It's basic functionality.
- **Unlimited swipes** — Artificially limited to push you toward paying.
- **Advanced filters** — A few extra dropdown menus that cost nothing to implement.
- **No ads** — They created the problem (ads) and sell you the solution (ad-free).

None of these features cost the company anything significant. They're digital switches. The only reason they're behind a paywall is because the company knows you'll eventually cave.

## The Hidden Cost of Paywalled Dating

When dating apps charge for basic features, they don't just take your money — they change who you meet:

- **The most active users are often the ones who can't afford premium.** Students, young professionals, people between jobs. Paywalls filter them out.
- **Premium users get priority visibility.** You're not seeing the best matches — you're seeing the ones who paid to be seen.
- **The algorithm optimizes for retention, not connection.** A successful match means two paying customers leave. The app's financial incentive is to keep you single and swiping.
