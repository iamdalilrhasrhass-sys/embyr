# How to Spot Fake Profiles and Catfish on Gay Dating Apps

Fake profiles are the plague of online dating. For gay men, they're especially common — scammers target our community knowing that stigma makes us less likely to report. Here's how to spot them before you waste your time (or worse).

## The 30-Second Fake Profile Test

Before you even reply, scan for these red flags:

### 1. Only One Photo (And It's a Model)

If a profile has exactly one photo and the guy looks like he walked off a magazine cover, it's almost certainly fake. Reverse image search the photo (Google Images, TinEye). Scammers steal photos from Instagram fitness influencers and OnlyFans creators.

### 2. "Can't Meet Right Now, But..."

They're working on an oil rig. They're in the military, deployed overseas. They're a doctor doing charity work in a remote village. Any excuse that prevents meeting in person is a massive red flag.

### 3. They Want to Move to WhatsApp/Telegram Immediately

Within 3-5 messages, they're pushing to move off the app. This is because dating apps have fraud detection — moving to an encrypted app lets scammers operate freely.

### 4. Broken Grammar That Doesn't Match the Photos

The profile photos show a 25-year-old in Paris, but the messages read like machine-translated romance scams. "Hello dear, I am very interest in you profile. You are handsome gentleman."

### 5. They Ask for Money (Eventually)

It starts small. "My phone broke, can you send €20 for a new SIM?" Then escalates. By the time you realize it's a scam, you've sent hundreds of euros to someone who doesn't exist.

## The Bots: Even Easier to Spot

Bots are automated profiles that send generic messages to drive traffic to external sites. Signs:

- Message arrives **instantly** after matching
- Contains a link (OnlyFans, suspicious "dating" sites, crypto)
- Profile bio is a single emoji or "follow me on IG @..."
- Never responds to specific questions

Report and block immediately. Don't engage — even clicking their link can expose your data.
